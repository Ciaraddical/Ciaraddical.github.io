What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?

1- a lot of my identity revolves around being seen to be smart - I find struggling to learn something really challenging

2- I find helping others learn really rewarding - it increases my self-image in a constructive way

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

Values - I feel that if we have a clear understanding of our values and motivations we can approach something - a project, or a problem, with more intent and self awareness. This aids in maintaining focus and encourages altruism

Empathy - one of the keys to successful cooperation, the ability to step into someone else’s shoes is also useful while learning - understanding the difference between how people learn also fosters self-compassion and a growth mindset.

Self-awareness - programming seems to almost invariably lead to self-awareness - we stare at code we imagine to be flawless for hours before someone else points out a mistake, and the realisation that we are viewing something with warped senses falls into place. I feel like core learning has filled gap in my knowledge that has been there for a while - ways to approach new knowledge, increase productivity, and deal with challenges - these were all things I had previously seen as somewhat hardwired.

It’s not a step you can really skip, if you want to progress and improve your capability to learn.

What does it mean to display inline vs inline blocks?

21 April 2022

These terms describe a way of arranging elements, such as paragraphs, images, or buttons, on a webpage. Inline elements, like links, will display on the same line on a website, and can have items either side. For example, this is an inline element. (With a handy dandy red border.) Inline elements are unable to be given heights, so that they won't disrupt the arrangement of the lines around them.

The opposite of inline elements are block elements. Block elements will always stack on top of each other (like a stack of toy blocks), even if there's room to fit side by side. They can be given height, and they will default to displaying 100% wide - taking up the whole page.

Here's an example of a block element, with that handy border. It's totally messed with the margins of the paragraph underneath, too - unwieldy bastard.
Elements have a default display mode - for example, paragraphs are block elements, because it makes sense for new paragraphs to be on a new line, and blocks will force a new line.

Inline-block takes attributes from both inline and block (as the name might suggest). Elements can be displayed on the same line, but their size can be modified to a much greater extent than inline elements. Explain a situation where you have had an ethical decision to make. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and the consequences of your action.

During the August lockdown in 2021, I decided to get back in touch with a friend from highschool - we’d had a bit of a friendship break up in our final year, but I was conscious of how much I’d changed in the four years since then, and I wanted to find out if she had also changed, and if there was an opportunity to reconnect.

We chatted for about two weeks, but then she stopped replying. This had been a common situation when we were in high school, and I got the sense that she was struggling a bit with anxiety and felt anxious about replying after too much time had passed. I had a lot of feelings about it, mostly tied up in how we’d behaved towards each other in school, and I ended up writing it all out in my diary. I wrote about how I wasn’t sure I could forgive her for some things, but I’d like to try forget them and start anew.

I ended up sending her the diary entry, so that she knew exactly how I felt. I got a lot of peace from being so explicitly open about how I felt, but I didn’t receive a reply. In the letter I’d also talked about how I planned to keep reaching out to her unless she actually asked me to stop, because I knew the reason she wasn’t replying was probably nothing to do with me and more about how she was feeling. I kept reaching out for maybe the next two weeks, but it took a bit of energy from me every time I extended myself and got no reply. Eventually, I sent a message saying that while I was always available if she needed something, I couldn’t keep reaching out like that. It was a hard decision to make - it felt like abandoning someone who probably needed support and a friend to talk to, but at the same time, I knew that it wasn’t a constructive thing for me personally to be doing - sometimes it felt like talking to a brick wall, and I was also struggling a bit with my own depression during lockdown. I’d done all I could but to keep trying with nothing changing seemed useless.

When I was back home for Christmas, I got in touch again and suggested we catch up in person. We grabbed a coffee together, and had a good chat, but didn’t touch on a lot of the stuff I’d mentioned in my letter. She told me the reason she’d stopped replying was due to some things happening in her own life and had nothing to do with me, and that she was really grateful I’d been so persistent. She told me she’d actually written a letter as a reply to mine but not felt confident enough to send it, then promised to send it when she got home.

When we parted, she stopped replying again. I didn’t receive the letter, and I was really disappointed. I’d got my hopes up that we would be friends again, but it didn’t happen. I still have no idea why that is, but I keep sending the odd message about a film I’ll watch or something I’ve seen, just in case she replies.

Although this seems contradictory to the previous choice I made, I think it aligns closer to my values of kindness and persistence. I no longer invest so much energy into these messages, or care too much about whether there’s a response, but I feel like I’m keeping my word, and hopefully when she’s ready she’ll reply. I feel like while I have the mental energy to be doing it, it’s the right thing to do, and it makes me feel better about myself.

Javascript Fundamentals

1 May 2022

How does Javascript relate to HTML and CSS?

A good analogy to describe the relationship between these three would be to imagine them as parts of a body. HTML is the bones, providing structure, the core upon which other things rest. Each bone or joint has a specific movement or range of motion, but they are limited in what they can do. We might say that CSS is like the skin, or perhaps the clothing on the body. Easily customisable, yet it doesn't affect the function of other parts (we'll leave the concept of skinny jeans out of this metaphor haha). Javascript, that beautiful beast, is like the brain. It it responsible for guiding the bones/HTML in what to do. Our bodies can do incredibly complex things, much of it beneath the surface. However, without our brains to drive this, nothing would be able to happen.

Explain control flow and loops, using an example process

Control flow refers to the order in which javascript is executed. This could be sequential (one thing after another), or it could be conditional - a loop that will run until it meets conditions that will allow it to stop. Several aspects of everyday life also conform to these orders. For example, we follow a recipe in a sequential order - the first steps must be completed in order to do the next. However, we might describe the process of cooking someting in the oven as conditional. That loop goes like this: Check on bread. If cooked, take out of oven. Else leave for ten more minutes. This loop could be repeated (and has been, in my kitchen) again and again, until it's allowed to stop (the bread is cooked).

Descibe what the DOM is and an example of how it could be interacted with

The DOM, short for Document Object Model, is a way of navigating and interacting with the parts that make up a web page (the "document"). This includes how different coding languages will affect each other. For example, you could use Javascript to continually update values written in a HTML file. Without the DOM, there would be no way to access the HTML in order to modify it using Javascript.

What's the difference between accessing data from arrays, vs from objects?

When we access data from arrays, we can use the index number of that data, keeping in mind that arrays will count data beginning with zero. This is great when we want, say, the fifth item in a list, or we want to access them all in order. When we access data from objects, however, we do it using their properties - we ask for information like name, or age. This is better if we need that specific information.

What are functions, and why are they helpful?

A function in Javascript is a series of instructions that will be carried out when that function is called, or invoked. This could be by typing code, or it could be an automatic response to an event like a mouse click or button press. Functions can be as simple or complex as you like - one of the most commonly used functions is console.log(), which prints whatever's inside the brackets to the console. Functions always follow the formula of name + round brackets, and inside the brackets is where we define parameters for the function. These parameters are referred to as arguments when we give them values when calling a function. These arguments could be numbers the function will add together, or information in the form of strings, or other types of values. Functions are one of the big building blocks of Javascript - often a series of very simple functions will combine to create something quite complex.

Emotional Intelligence

25 April 2022

What is Emotional Intelligence?
When we describe someone as emotionally intelligent, we’re referring to their ability to recognise their emotions and those of others. Although we can’t always control what emotions we feel, being aware of them can be the first step to regulating them. Empathy is far easier once we are able to understand what others may be feeling - this also helps judge the mood of groups. We are influenced by the emotions and actions of those around us, so being able to read a room, or pick up on potential conflicts before they escalate, is a good skill to have.
Our emotional intelligence often stems from our parents’ behaviour - the first people to model social interactions for us when we’re young - even if we were too young to consciously remember. However, “EQ” can also be taught. Anger management classes are a good example of this -teaching how to recognise our emotions and find less harmful ways of dealing with them.

How is it different to IQ?
IQ is widely considered to test how “smart” someone is. However, it tests a limited field of knowledge - mostly analytical abilities and logical problem solving. IQ tests can also have the flaw of being biased towards test-takers with a particular upbringing - such as growing up within a western culture. It’s hard to test for knowledge people “should know”, such as current politicians, or historical events, without falling into this bias.
It’s widely accepted nowadays that, although useful, this is not the only type of knowledge with value - working well with others, and even navigating most facets of everyday life, are made easier if you are able to empathise and relate to others - social, or emotional intelligence.

Why is it important?
Although humans are capable of incredible things individually, I feel like our greatest achievements result from many people working together. However, in order to work together effectively, we need more than just IQ. Emotional intelligence can optimise our social interactions, play a vital role in self-development, and result in higher productivity.
Often I would prefer to be around someone who is easy to get along with (aka emotionally intelligent) rather than someone with a high IQ. Even if I’m working on a problem with someone else with a really high IQ, if they don’t have the skills to effectively communicate their ideas in a way I’ll understand, it’ll be really hard to work together. I know that if I feel this way, it’s likely others do too, including people I might like to be employed by.

Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

2 May 2022

Discuss how understanding the priciples of neuroplasticity benefits people

To understand the principles of neuroplasticity is to understand that we are capable of learning anything at any time - widespread ideas around “teaching old dogs new tricks”, notions of having an “artistic brain” or a “logical brain”, or of being smart vs slow, are mistaken.

Our brains are not static - they adapt and grow as we use them, to be more efficient. Our knowledge is not fixed - we can train our brains to do whatever we want, through practice and conscious effort. The pathways of neurons in our brains are constantly being rewired, to support whatever we are using our brain for. This means that the more often we do something, the stronger those pathways become. This is also why things become easier over time, as these pathways become established.

The understanding of these principles is important - they help to eliminate the self-critical thoughts that often come up while struggling to learn new things. “I’ll never be good at this”, “This is so new to me, I’ll never understand it”, “My brain just doesn’t work this way” - these statements are scientifically disproved by numerous studies on the nature of neuroplasticity.

Discuss how you might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit.

For me, I frequently associate the feeling of struggling to learn something with failure - I’m very used to having things come quickly to me. To realise that the feeling of grappling with something is actually the feeling of my brain creating new neural pathways, is a far more inspiring way to look at things.

The idea of salience affecting neuroplasticity also confirms something I’ve been suspecting lately - I find it easier to learn and stay engaged with something if it’s relevant to things I’m interested in, or if I can see the possible uses for it. In terms of learning to code, this means that I often understand something better after seeing it in context - although I’ll think I understand it after an isolated example, I get a lot more clarity after seeing it used, and I remember it better.

What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

Regular practice of a new skill, such as coding. Skills need to be used, or the neural pathways will degrade, and new skills particularly so. Repetition helps reinforce these pathways - the more a skill is used, the better we get at it.

Specifically practice what you want to get good at. It’s not enough to be on the computer all day - if I want to become good at coding, I need to be practicing coding. There’s not really a shortcut.

Practice also needs to be at the right level of intensity - the goldilocks zone which sits between the comfort zone and the panic zone - also known as the learning zone. We need to be challenged in order to learn, and the more challenging the tasks are, the faster we’ll learn, to a point. There’s got to be a certain level of comprehension in order to move forward, obviously, but when you are struggling, that’s the feeling of learning.

    Problem Solving

    9 May 2022

    On being blocked on a simple problem:

    I feel like after this week I'm well qualified to talk about being blocked - a lot of time was spent in this state. The example that immediately springs to mind is a section of the Gradebook problem.

    The problem was to write a function that would average multiple scores and return one value. This seemed like a straightforward problem at the outset, and I had a clear idea of how to go about it. I used psuedocode to outline my approach, and then began writing the function. However, I kept encountering the error message "TypeError: Cannot read 'length' of undefined". My immediate reaction was to assume I had forgot something - some kind of connection between either the students' names and their scores, or that I wasn't accessing the scores in the right way.

    I think I spent almost an hour trying to get to the bottom of this - although I was aware I should ask for help if I was stuck for a long time, I didn't really consider myself stuck. I felt like I could work out where I'd gone wrong through a process of trial and error, and I just had to keep trying possible solutions. When I finally realised I wasn't really getting anywhere, I compared code with a peer who was stuck on the same problem. I was able to spot what was wrong with her code, and she was able to pass the tests and solve the problem. This confused me even more.

    To my eyes, our code was almost identical, and now I knew I had the right approach, but my code was still throwing the same error message. However when my peer read over my code, she was almost immediately able to spot what I'd missed - I'd written a lowercase 's' where it should've been uppercase. A single character, derailing the entire averaging function I'd written. It was humbling to say the least. However, it drove home a valuable lesson - the value of double and triple checking spelling and syntax, before assuming the problem is more complex than it actually is. Had I checked my spelling at the beginning, I might have saved myself a considerable amount of time and frustration. As it was, after an hour of staring at the same code, there was little chance I'd be able to spot something out of place. I was seeing what I wanted to see - perfectly normal code, and I thought that meant something else must be wrong. Lesson learnt, for now, although I don't doubt I'll repeat the error in the future at least once or twice.

    On elegantly solving a problem:

    The best example I can think of for this took place during the built-in methods challenge, while writing a capitalise function which would make the first letter of every word in a phrase start with a capital. At this stage of the built-in methods challenge, I was beginning to get an idea of which methods (common functions JavaScript has prewritten for people to use) would best suit each problem.

    Like the previous example, I began by outlining my approach in pseudocode. However I also included comments throughout the function, to help me keep track of what I was doing - the function involved converting back and forth between different data types, and I knew I didn't want to get mixed up about what I was working with. After writing my first attempt, I was reasonably sure I had solved the problem in the most efficient way, however it failed the tests. After checking my spelling and syntax and finding no faults, I googled the main matter I had some doubts about - whether I could use methods specific to a data type, within methods for other types.     The Story of Te Houtaewa

    E kore e mau i a koe, he wae kai pakiaka. A foot accustomed to running over roots makes the speediest runner. (Optional SUB title)

    20 April 2022

    Te Houtaewa was the fastest runner of his day and played many pranks on his peoples enemies. One morning his mother wanted kumara for the hangi (earth oven) and asked Te Houtaewa to go to the gardens at Te Kao, a short distance away. He agreed to fetch the kumara and told his mother to prepare the hangi.

    Instead of going to the nearby gardens, Te Houtaewa set off for Ahipara as he wanted to annoy some Te Rarawa people who lived there. Carrying two large baskets for the kumara, he ran like the wind, completing the journey over the hard sands of Te Oneroa a Tohe (Ninety Mile Beach) in the few hours it takes a good hangi to heat up properly. On reaching Ahipara, he went straight to the people's kumara pataka (storehouse for the sweet potatoes) situated at the foot of the hill Whangatauatia.

    While Te Houtaewa was filling his baskets with kumara he was spotted stealing the kai (food). He was immediately recognised.
